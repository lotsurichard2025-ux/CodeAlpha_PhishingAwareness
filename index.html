Four signals to check every time

01 — SENDER

Domain mismatch

Check the actual email address, not the display name. Look for extra characters, swapped letters, or a wrong top-level domain.

02 — LINKS

Hover before you click

The visible text and the real destination URL are often different. Hovering (desktop) or long-pressing (mobile) reveals the true link.

03 — TONE

Manufactured urgency

Threats of account closure, legal action, or "limited time" offers are designed to bypass careful thinking.

04 — REQUEST

Unusual ask

Legitimate organizations rarely ask you to confirm a password, send a gift card code, or "verify" full card details by email.

Social engineering tactics behind the message

  • AuthorityImpersonating a bank, employer, or government agency so the request feels legitimate.
  • UrgencyCreating a deadline so the target reacts emotionally instead of verifying.
  • FearThreatening account loss, fines, or legal trouble to provoke immediate compliance.
  • Curiosity"You have a pending delivery" or "someone shared a file with you" — bait designed to make you click.
  • PretextingBuilding a believable fake scenario (IT support, HR, a vendor) to justify an unusual request.

Best-practice checklist

  1. Verify the sender's exact email domain before trusting the message.
  2. Never click a link in an unsolicited email — type the known URL directly instead.
  3. Treat urgency and threats as a red flag, not a reason to rush.
  4. Enable multi-factor authentication so a leaked password isn't enough on its own.
  5. Report suspicious emails to IT / security instead of just deleting them.
